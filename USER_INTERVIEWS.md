## Interview 1 — Rajan Kumar
**Role:** Full Stack Developer, Final Year CSE Student  
**Context:** Student developer / Early-stage builder at IIIT Sonepat  
**LinkedIn:** [linkedin.com/in/rajan-kumar-b787382b9](https://linkedin.com/in/rajan-kumar-b787382b9)

I spoke with Rajan Kumar, my classmate from IIIT Sonepat, who actively works on full-stack development projects and frequently experiments with AI coding tools.

### Direct Quotes
> “The idea is actually crazy good because startups usually don’t even realize how much they’re overspending on AI tools.”

> “I liked that users can directly run the audit without friction. No forced signup before value is shown.”

> “Your UI is already good, but you can improve detailing and micro-interactions to make it feel more premium.”

> “Keep the pricing affordable so small startups quickly choose your product over competitors.”

### Most Surprising Insight
The most surprising thing Rajan mentioned was that reducing friction matters more than adding features initially. He specifically appreciated that the audit can run before asking for an email because most users abandon products that gate value too early.

### What This Changed in My Design
After this conversation, I became more confident about keeping the **“no-login-first”** experience. I also started focusing more on UI polish, animations, spacing, and micro-interactions because perception matters heavily in SaaS products targeting startup founders.

## Interview 2 — Ankur Gautam
**Role:** Co-Founder at Managion, Final Year CSE Student  
**Context:** Early-stage startup founder at IIIT Sonepat  
**LinkedIn:** [linkedin.com/in/ankur-gautam-0382b42b4](https://linkedin.com/in/ankur-gautam-0382b42b4)

I spoke with Ankur Gautam, my classmate from IIIT Sonepat and the co-founder of Managion. Since his startup is currently in a growth phase, I wanted to understand whether AI spend optimization would realistically matter to founders at this stage.

### Direct Quotes
> “This is a really beautiful idea. We can probably integrate something like this into our startup later.”

> “Right now we are still in the early growing stage, so AI cost optimization is not our biggest pain point yet.”

> “I checked your GitHub and your deployed website too — the project looks crazy good.”

> “Once our startup scales more, this type of tool becomes much more useful.”

### Most Surprising Insight
The surprising part was that early-stage startups often care more about speed and growth than optimization. Cost optimization becomes important only after operational spending starts scaling.

### What This Changed in My Design
This changed how I thought about **positioning the product**. Instead of targeting every startup, I started focusing more on teams already spending heavily on AI tools. It also pushed me to design the product as a long-term infrastructure optimization tool rather than only a simple calculator.

## Interview 3 — Yojit Goula
**Role:** Machine Learning Developer, Final Year CSE Student  
**Context:** Independent AI/ML Developer at IIIT Sonepat  
**LinkedIn:** [linkedin.com/in/yojit-goula-a8720a28a](https://linkedin.com/in/yojit-goula-a8720a28a)

I spoke with Yojit Goula, my classmate from IIIT Sonepat, who actively works on machine learning and AI-based development projects. Since he regularly experiments with different LLM APIs and AI tooling ecosystems, his feedback was very practical and technically grounded.

### Direct Quotes
> “Most developers keep paying for tools without comparing actual usage versus value.”

> “A tool that clearly explains where money is getting wasted would genuinely help teams.”

> “The shareable audit report idea is smart because founders love showing optimization wins publicly.”

> “Your strongest feature is not the calculations — it’s making complex pricing understandable.”

### Most Surprising Insight
The most surprising thing Yojit mentioned was that many technical users do not fully understand their own AI infrastructure costs because pricing models across vendors are confusing and constantly changing.

### What This Changed in My Design
This discussion pushed me to make the **audit explanations simpler and more visual** instead of only showing raw numbers. I also started thinking more seriously about transparent reasoning and defensible calculations so users trust the recommendations.
